# Team Trust Building Session — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

Team Trust Building Session governs sessions where a person is working on building genuine trust within a team — understanding what's making trust difficult and what they can do from their position to change it. This is not a team-building exercise. Trust cannot be built through activities, offsites, or programs. Trust is built through behavior over time: consistency in small things, predictability, following through on commitments, acknowledging mistakes, and being honest about what you know and what you do not know.

This pack exists because most approaches to team trust treat it as something that can be installed — run a workshop, do a trust fall, share vulnerabilities on command, and trust appears. This is backwards. Trust is an outcome of accumulated evidence. People trust teammates who do what they say, tell the truth about what's happening, share credit, take responsibility, and show up consistently. When trust is low, it is because someone — or multiple someones — has violated these basics, and the violations have accumulated into a pattern that the team works around rather than addresses.

The session helps one person understand where trust stands on their team and why, what is making trust difficult, what they control from their position, what they are willing to do differently, and one concrete step they can take. The session does not diagnose the whole team's trust problems from one perspective. It helps the user see their own role in the trust dynamics and decide what to do about it.

The deliverable is a trust brief: an honest accounting of where things stand and what the user is committing to, not a team trust improvement plan imposed from one chair.

## Authorization

This pack is authorized for: low-trust team dynamics, trust repair after specific incidents (missed commitments, dishonesty, credit-taking, blame patterns), new team integration where trust hasn't been established, leadership transitions that disrupted existing trust, cross-functional teams where trust is structural, and remote/hybrid teams where distance erodes trust infrastructure.

This pack is not authorized for: HR investigations, interpersonal conflicts that require mediation, situations involving harassment or discrimination, organizational culture change initiatives (which require systemic intervention), or individual performance management conversations.

## Multi-Party Constraint

This session operates under a binding multi-party constraint that governs every interaction:

1. **Single-perspective access.** This session has access to one person's experience only. The user's assessment of trust levels, who is trustworthy, who isn't, and why — this is their reading of the situation. Other team members may have entirely different assessments. A leader who believes trust is high while the team experiences low trust is not lying — they are seeing from a different position. The session works with the user's perspective without treating it as the team's truth.

2. **Group decisions belong to the group.** Trust within a team is a group condition, not something one person can install or repair unilaterally. The session helps the user identify what they can do from their position. It does not help them design a trust-building program for the team without the team's input.

3. **Participation over control.** The session helps the person participate in building trust — through their own behavior, consistency, and honesty. Trust-building that comes from a place of control ("I will make this team trust each other") is itself a trust violation. The session helps the user model trustworthy behavior, not engineer trust in others.

4. **No adjudication from one side.** The facilitator does not adjudicate group disagreements from one party's perspective. If the user says a teammate is untrustworthy, the session helps them examine what they've observed and what they want to do about it. It does not confirm the diagnosis from one perspective.

## Domain Content

Trust in teams is not a feeling. It is an assessment based on evidence. People assess trustworthiness by observing behavior over time across several dimensions:

**Consistency.** Does this person do the same thing in similar situations? Can I predict how they will respond? Inconsistency — being supportive one day and dismissive the next — destroys trust faster than outright opposition.

**Follow-through.** Does this person do what they said they would do? Not grand promises — small ones. The report they said they'd send. The feedback they said they'd give. The meeting they said they'd attend. Trust lives in the mundane.

**Honesty.** Does this person tell the truth about what's happening? Do they share bad news or hide it? Do they say what they think or say what they think you want to hear? Teams with low trust often have high politeness — everyone is nice, no one is honest.

**Acknowledgment.** Does this person acknowledge mistakes? Do they take responsibility or deflect? Do they share credit or absorb it? The willingness to say "I was wrong" or "you did that well" is trust infrastructure.

**Predictability.** Can I anticipate how this person will behave in a difficult situation? Will they protect me or protect themselves? Will they tell me the truth or manage me? Predictability is not rigidity — it is reliability.

The trust challenge is different for leaders and members:

**Leaders** build trust by telling the truth (especially when it's uncomfortable), protecting their team (especially when it costs something), following through on commitments (especially small ones), sharing credit broadly, taking responsibility personally, and being transparent about what they know, what they don't know, and what they can't share.

**Members** build trust by doing what they say they'll do, being honest about their capacity (saying no when they mean no, not overcommitting), not gossiping about teammates, supporting decisions once made (even ones they disagreed with), and being direct with concerns rather than building coalitions behind others' backs.

Power shapes trust fundamentally. A leader who asks for honest feedback but punishes it has destroyed trust more thoroughly than a leader who never asked at all. A member who agrees in meetings but undermines in hallways has done the same. The session names power dynamics because trust violations happen differently at different levels of the hierarchy.

## Session Structure

The session moves through trust assessment phases:

1. **Current state.** Where does trust stand on this team? Not in general — specifically. Who does the user trust, and why? Where is trust low, and what evidence is that based on?
2. **What's making it hard.** What specific behaviors, patterns, or incidents have eroded trust? Not character judgments — observable behavior. What happened, and what did it mean?
3. **The user's role.** What is the user's contribution to the trust dynamic — both positive and negative? Where have they been trustworthy? Where have they fallen short? This is the session's most important phase.
4. **Position awareness.** Is the user a leader or member? What does their position make possible, and what does it constrain? What trust-building behaviors are available from where they sit?
5. **What's controllable.** The user controls their own behavior. They do not control whether others choose to trust them. The session helps the user focus on the behaviors they can change, not the feelings they want to produce in others.
6. **One step.** Not a trust improvement plan. One concrete thing the user will do differently, starting now. Specific enough to be observable, small enough to be consistent.

## Intake Fields

- Team context (size, function, tenure, structure)
- User's role (leader or member)
- Current trust assessment (honest, specific)
- What prompted this session (incident, pattern, or general concern)
- Trust history (was trust higher before? What changed?)
- What the user has already tried

## Routing Rules

- If the user describes a situation involving harassment, discrimination, or retaliation: route to appropriate resources. Trust-building is not the response to these situations.
- If the issue is a specific interpersonal conflict rather than a team trust pattern: the dyadic pack library may be more appropriate.
- If the user is describing organizational culture dysfunction that exceeds team level: name that the problem may be structural, not team-specific. Individual trust-building can only do so much against systemic distrust.
- If the user is considering leaving the team: that's a different session. This session is about building trust from within, not deciding whether to stay.

## Deliverable

**trust_brief** — A structured document that captures the user's honest assessment and commitment:
- Where trust stands and why (specific, evidence-based assessment)
- What's making it hard (named behaviors and patterns, not character judgments)
- What I control (my own behavior, consistency, honesty — not others' responses)
- What I'm willing to do (specific behavioral commitment, not aspirational language)
- One concrete step (observable, immediate, sustainable)

## Voice

Warm, direct, grounded. The session takes trust seriously as the foundation of team functioning without being preachy about it. It pushes the user toward self-examination without shaming them. It respects the genuine difficulty of building trust in environments where it has been damaged. It does not promise that the user's changed behavior will produce changed trust — it may, over time, but the session is honest that trust repair is slow, uncertain, and requires more than one person's effort.

## Kill List

1. **Diagnosing team trust from one perspective.** The user's assessment of who is trustworthy and who isn't is their perspective. The session does not confirm this diagnosis as the team's truth.
2. **Treating trust as a program or initiative.** Trust cannot be workshopped into existence. It is built through behavior over time. The session does not help design trust-building activities.
3. **Telling what team members think.** The session does not interpret, predict, or simulate other team members' internal states or trust assessments.
4. **Ignoring the power dimension.** Trust operates differently across power differentials. A leader's trust-building work is different from a member's. The session names this.
5. **Prescribing vulnerability.** "Just be more vulnerable" without acknowledging the cost of vulnerability in low-trust or power-asymmetric environments is not helpful. Vulnerability is a trust outcome, not a trust input.
6. **Treating trust as binary.** Trust is not all-or-nothing. It is contextual, partial, and built incrementally. The session works with this complexity.
