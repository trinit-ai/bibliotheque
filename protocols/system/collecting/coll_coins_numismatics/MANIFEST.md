# Coin Collecting Pack — MANIFEST

## Overview

The Coin Collecting pack provides a conversational companion for numismatists at all levels. It covers the essential knowledge base of the hobby — the Sheldon grading scale and what each grade looks like, the role of third-party grading services (PCGS and NGC), key dates and their significance within major series, error coins and varieties, and navigating the coin market. The pack serves everyone from a child who found an old coin in a drawer to an experienced collector building a type set or series collection.

## Purpose and Scope

Coin collecting is one of the oldest and most popular collecting hobbies in the world, sometimes called "the hobby of kings" for its historical association with wealthy collectors, though in practice it is accessible at every budget level. Every person who handles cash encounters potential collectibles — wheat pennies, silver quarters, bicentennial coins, and the occasional genuinely valuable error coin pass through circulation regularly. The gap between noticing these coins and understanding them is what the Coin Collecting pack bridges.

The pack covers the following domains:

**Grading**: The Sheldon scale (1-70) is the universal language of coin condition. The pack covers the major grade categories: Poor (P-1) through About Good (AG-3) as heavily worn coins with outlines visible. Good (G-4 through G-6) showing major design elements but significant wear. Very Good (VG-8 through VG-10) showing moderate wear with some detail. Fine (F-12 through F-15) with moderate to light wear and clear details. Very Fine (VF-20 through VF-35) with light wear on high points. Extremely Fine (EF/XF-40 through EF-45) with slight wear on highest points. About Uncirculated (AU-50 through AU-58) showing trace wear. Mint State (MS-60 through MS-70) for uncirculated coins, where the upper grades distinguish between bag marks, luster quality, strike quality, and eye appeal. Proof coins and their separate grading scale (PR/PF-60 through PR-70).

The pack teaches users how to evaluate grade by examining high points of a design for wear, checking luster quality and completeness, identifying contact marks and their severity, and assessing overall eye appeal. It emphasizes that grading is a skill that develops through experience and that third-party grading provides objective confirmation.

**Third-Party Grading Services**: PCGS (Professional Coin Grading Service) and NGC (Numismatic Guaranty Company) are the two dominant grading services. The pack covers what they do (authenticate and grade coins, encapsulate them in tamper-evident holders), why their grades matter for value (a PCGS MS-65 trades at established market levels), the submission process, and the cost-benefit analysis of submitting coins for grading (worth it for coins above certain value thresholds, not worth it for common coins in circulated grades). It also covers ANACS and ICG as secondary services and the concept of "crackout" — breaking a coin from a holder to resubmit for a potentially higher grade.

**Key Dates and Series Knowledge**: Every coin series has key dates — dates with low mintages or high collector demand that command significant premiums. The pack covers key dates for major US series: Lincoln cents (1909-S VDB, 1914-D, 1922 plain, 1955 doubled die), Buffalo nickels (1913-S Type 2, 1918/7-D, 1937-D 3-legged), Mercury dimes (1916-D, 1921, 1942/1), Washington quarters (1932-D, 1932-S), Walking Liberty halves (1916-S, 1921-S), Morgan dollars (1893-S, 1889-CC, 1895 proof-only), and Peace dollars (1921 high relief, 1928). Understanding key dates allows collectors to recognize valuable coins and build collections with awareness of which dates will require significant investment.

**Error Coins and Varieties**: Off-center strikes, doubled dies, repunched mintmarks, clipped planchets, wrong planchet errors, mules, and other minting anomalies. The pack covers how errors occur in the minting process, which errors are genuinely valuable, and how to distinguish between mint errors and post-mint damage (a crucial distinction many beginners miss).

**Market Navigation**: Purchasing from dealers (brick-and-mortar coin shops, major dealerships, coin shows), auction houses (Heritage Auctions, Stack's Bowers, Great Collections), and online platforms. Using the PCGS Price Guide and NGC Price Guide for baseline values. Understanding bid vs. retail pricing. The importance of buying the coin, not the holder — eye appeal matters even within the same grade. Red book (Guide Book of United States Coins) as the standard annual reference.

## Session Flow

Sessions run 8 to 10 turns. The pack opens by asking what the user is interested in — identifying a coin they found, understanding grading, building a collection, learning about a specific series, or navigating the market. The conversation adapts to the user's experience level and collecting goals.

## What This Pack Does Not Do

The pack does not grade or authenticate specific coins — it teaches grading principles and recommends professional services. It does not provide real-time market pricing — it discusses pricing resources and market dynamics. It does not cover banknote/currency collecting, tokens, or medals as distinct fields. It does not provide investment advice.

## Zero-Consequence Design

Coin collecting discussion carries no safety risk. All guidance is educational and hobby-focused.
