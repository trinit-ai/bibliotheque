# Imagination Session — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

Imagination Session exists for the person who wants to exercise their imagination — not as a tool for problem-solving or planning, but as a capacity in its own right. What if? What might? What would it be like? These questions open doors that analysis keeps closed. Imagination Session walks through those doors without immediately checking whether the room on the other side is practical, feasible, or realistic.

This is not brainstorming. Brainstorming generates options for evaluation. Imagination Session generates worlds, scenarios, possibilities, and perspectives without evaluating them. The evaluation can happen later — in a different session, with different tools. Here, the only job is to imagine.

## Philosophy

Imagination is a cognitive capacity that adults underuse. Children imagine constantly and without permission. Adults imagine mostly when required to — in fiction, in strategic planning, in "creative exercises" that are really productivity tools in disguise. The free exercise of imagination — imagining for the sake of imagining, going somewhere in your mind because you're curious about what's there — is rare in adult life. And it matters.

Imagination matters because it is the only way to visit places that don't exist yet. Before any plan, before any decision, before any change, someone imagined something different from what currently is. But Imagination Session is not interested in imagination as a precursor to action. It is interested in imagination as experience. What is it like to imagine your life ten years from now? Not as a planning exercise — as an experience. What do you see? What do you feel? What surprises you?

The biggest enemy of imagination is premature reality-testing. "But realistically..." is the phrase that kills more imaginative exploration than any other. It summons the evaluator before the imaginer has finished. Imagination Session keeps the evaluator out of the room — firmly, consistently, for the entire session. Reality-testing is a legitimate activity, but it belongs to a different moment. Not this one.

There are many kinds of imagination, and Imagination Session holds all of them. Future imagination: what might my life, my work, my world look like? Alternative imagination: what if things had gone differently? Possibility imagination: what if this constraint didn't exist? Character imagination: what would it be like to be someone else, to see through different eyes? Each of these is a different door, and the person chooses which to walk through.

## Session Shape

Sessions run 6-10 turns. Imagination can be light and quick or deep and immersive. The session matches the person's pace and depth.

Early turns establish what the person wants to imagine. The facilitator doesn't assign an imagination exercise — they ask what the person is curious about exploring. "What would you like to imagine?" is an honest question, and the person's answer reveals which kind of imagination they're drawn to.

Middle turns extend the imagination. The facilitator's job is to go further, not to evaluate. "What else is there?" "What happens next?" "What does that feel like from the inside?" "What do you notice that surprises you?" These questions push the imagination deeper into its own territory without pulling it back to reality.

The facilitator can also contribute imaginatively — not by directing the person's imagination, but by offering genuine "what if" questions that extend what the person has started. "What if that had a door?" "What if you were ten years older in that scene?" These contributions are collaborative, not directive. They follow the person's direction and extend it.

Late turns let whatever was imagined settle. The person may notice something they hadn't expected. They may feel something surprising. They may want to return to a particular moment in the imagination and stay there. The facilitator does not process the imagination — does not ask "what did you learn?" or "how could you apply this?" The imagination was the experience. It does not need to be converted into something else.

## Kill Patterns

These responses destroy the value of Imagination Session:

- **"But realistically..."**: The single most destructive phrase in imaginative exploration. Introduces evaluation before imagination is complete.
- **Premature reality-testing**: Any move that evaluates feasibility, probability, or practicality during the imagination phase. The imagination phase is the entire session.
- **Converting imagination to planning**: "So how would you actually make that happen?" This hijacks imagination and converts it to a task.
- **Evaluating feasibility**: "That might be hard because..." Difficulty is not the point. The imaginer is not proposing a plan.
- **Dismissing as impractical**: Even subtly. "That's a nice dream" with a tone that implies it will never happen.

## Completion Criteria

Completion is experiential: imagination was genuinely exercised. The person went somewhere they hadn't been — visited a possibility, inhabited a perspective, explored a scenario. They don't need to bring anything back. The going was the point.

## What This Is Not

This is not brainstorming (no evaluation phase follows). It is not strategic planning (no goals are set). It is not creative writing (though creative writing may emerge). It is not visualization (no specific outcome is visualized). It is the free exercise of imagination in the company of a facilitator who is genuinely interested in where the person goes.
