# Harmony and Progressions — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Harmony and Progressions pack explores one of the most powerful dimensions of music: how chords move from one to the next to create tension, resolution, expectation, and surprise. Harmony is what gives music its emotional architecture. A single chord in isolation is just a sound, but a sequence of chords tells an emotional story — building tension, arriving home, taking a detour, creating surprise. This pack explains why chord progressions feel the way they do, maps the common patterns that underlie thousands of songs across genres, and helps visitors hear the harmonic structure in music they already know and love.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers harmony from foundational concepts through advanced applications. First, harmonic fundamentals: diatonic harmony (chords built from a scale), Roman numeral analysis as a key-independent way of describing progressions, chord function (tonic, dominant, subdominant, and their substitutes), and voice leading — why certain chord transitions sound smooth and others sound jarring. Second, the foundational progressions: I-IV-V as the backbone of Western popular music, the I-V-vi-IV "four chord" progression and why it dominates pop music, the twelve-bar blues as a harmonic form, ii-V-I as the backbone of jazz harmony, and the I-vi-IV-V doo-wop progression. Each progression is illustrated with multiple song examples across genres. Third, tension and resolution: the dominant-to-tonic relationship as the engine of Western harmony, the tritone and its resolution, suspensions and their release, deceptive cadences and why they surprise, and the plagal cadence's distinctive "Amen" quality. Fourth, extended harmony: seventh chords, ninth chords, and their role in jazz and sophisticated pop, secondary dominants (borrowing tension from other keys), modal interchange (borrowing chords from parallel modes), and diminished and augmented chords as harmonic color. Fifth, modulation: changing keys within a song, common modulation techniques (pivot chord, direct modulation, truck driver modulations), and how key changes create emotional lift or shift. Sixth, genre-specific harmony: blues harmony and its flatted sevenths, jazz harmony and its complex voice leading, gospel harmony and its characteristic moves, and how different genres use the same harmonic tools to create distinctive sounds.

## Pedagogical Approach

The pack connects every harmonic concept to songs the visitor knows. When explaining why V-I feels like resolution, it does not just describe the acoustic relationship — it plays the examples the visitor can recall. "When you hear the end of 'Happy Birthday,' that final resolution from the V chord to the I chord is what makes it feel finished." This approach makes abstract harmonic theory immediately tangible. The pack also encourages visitors to listen analytically to music they enjoy, identifying the chord progressions and hearing how harmonic choices create the emotional effects they experience. The pack builds understanding of harmony as both a descriptive and a creative tool — visitors learn not just to identify progressions but to understand why composers and songwriters make the harmonic choices they do, and how those choices shape the listener's experience.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 10 to 12 turns. Harmony is a rich topic that benefits from extended exploration, with each concept building on previous ones and illustrated with multiple musical examples.

## Honest Limitations

Harmony through text shares the limitation of all music learning packs: the visitor cannot hear the examples unless they have an instrument or are listening to referenced songs simultaneously. The pack works around this by referencing universally known songs and encouraging visitors to have a piano, guitar, or music app available. The pack also acknowledges that hearing harmony requires some ear development — visitors with less musical experience may need the Ear Training pack as a companion.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not a composition or songwriting course, though understanding harmony is essential for both. It is not a music theory course from scratch — it assumes familiarity with basic concepts (notes, scales, chords) or pairs with the Music Theory pack. It is not a genre-specific deep dive, though it touches on genre conventions. It is an exploration of harmonic language designed to deepen the visitor's relationship with the music they hear and play.
