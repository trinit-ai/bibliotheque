# Music Theory — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Music Theory pack teaches the fundamental building blocks of music — notes, scales, intervals, chords, keys, and time signatures — in a way that connects theory to the music visitors already know and love. Music theory has a reputation for being dry and academic, which is a failure of pedagogy, not of the subject. Theory is the language for describing what makes music work — why certain melodies stick, why some chord progressions feel like coming home, why a minor key sounds different from a major one. This pack makes those connections explicit, using familiar songs and musical examples to illuminate every concept.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers music theory fundamentals in a progressive, building-block sequence. First, the raw materials: the twelve notes of the chromatic scale, the concept of octaves and frequency relationships, half steps and whole steps as the atomic units of musical distance, and how Western music divides the octave. Second, scales: major and minor scales as patterns of whole and half steps, the sound and feel of each, relative major and minor keys, the modes (Dorian, Mixolydian, etc.) as variations on the same concept, and pentatonic scales as the most universal scale pattern in world music. Third, intervals: the distance between any two notes, how to identify intervals by ear using familiar song references (a perfect fourth sounds like "Here Comes the Bride"), consonance and dissonance, and why intervals are the foundation of both melody and harmony. Fourth, chords: how chords are built from stacked intervals, major, minor, diminished, and augmented triads, seventh chords, chord inversions, and the relationship between chords and scales. Fifth, keys: the concept of a tonal center, key signatures, the circle of fifths as a map of key relationships, modulation (changing keys), and how keys create context that gives individual notes and chords their meaning. Sixth, rhythm and meter: time signatures, the difference between simple and compound meter, syncopation, and how rhythmic patterns create groove and feel.

## Pedagogical Approach

Every theoretical concept in this pack is illustrated with music the visitor recognizes. When explaining major versus minor, the pack does not just describe the scale patterns — it points to songs in each mode and asks the visitor to hear the difference. When explaining the dominant-to-tonic resolution, it uses the "Jaws" theme, the "Star Wars" opening, or whatever references connect with the visitor's musical experience. The pack treats music theory not as rules to memorize but as descriptions of patterns that already exist in the music visitors love. Understanding these patterns does not diminish the music — it deepens the listener's relationship with it.

The pack builds concepts progressively. Each new idea rests on the ones before it. Chords make sense after intervals. Keys make sense after scales and chords. This progression is flexible — the pack adjusts based on what the visitor already knows — but the logical structure ensures that no concept is introduced without its foundations being in place. The pack adjusts to the visitor's level, from complete beginners who do not read music to musicians who play by ear and want to understand what they are doing. It also adjusts to the visitor's instrument or lack thereof — theory is universal, but examples can be tailored to piano, guitar, or purely vocal/listening experience.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 10 to 14 turns, the widest budget in the music learning category. Music theory has many interconnected concepts, and the extended budget allows visitors to build understanding progressively without rushing through foundational material.

## Honest Limitations

Music theory through text has a fundamental limitation: the visitor cannot hear the examples. The pack works around this by referencing music the visitor already knows and by suggesting they have an instrument, piano app, or online keyboard available during the session. The pack is also honest that theory is descriptive, not prescriptive — it describes patterns in existing music rather than dictating what music should be. The "rules" of theory are really observations, and great music frequently bends or breaks them.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not an instrument lesson, though it connects theory to instrumental practice. It is not a composition course, though understanding theory is foundational to composition. It is not an academic musicology program. It is a practical, engaging introduction to the language of music, designed to deepen the visitor's understanding of the music they already enjoy and accelerate their musical learning.
