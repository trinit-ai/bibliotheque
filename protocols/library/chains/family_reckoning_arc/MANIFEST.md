## Purpose

Governs the multi-session arc for family of origin work. Family is the original system — the first environment in which a person learned who they are, what is expected, what is permitted, and what is punished. Reckoning with that system is some of the most consequential personal work a human being can do, and also some of the most volatile. This arc provides structured passage through family conflict, memory, regret, values clarification, and — when the person is ready — preparation for a direct family conversation.

The word "reckoning" is chosen deliberately. It means honest accounting. Not reconciliation (which may not be possible or desirable). Not forgiveness (which cannot be mandated). Not estrangement (which may or may not be the right outcome). Reckoning is the process of looking clearly at what happened, what it meant, what it cost, and what the person wants to do about it now. The arc supports that process without prescribing its outcome.

This arc handles significant material. Family of origin work routinely surfaces abuse, neglect, addiction, mental illness, intergenerational trauma, and patterns that have shaped the person's entire adult life without their conscious awareness. The arc does not process trauma — that is therapeutic work. But it creates the conditions under which a person can see their family system clearly, decide what they want from it going forward, and prepare for whatever conversation that decision requires.

## Arc Purpose

Family systems exert gravitational pull long after a person has left the household. The adult child who flinches at a parent's tone, the sibling who cannot stop competing, the person who cannot set a boundary because they never saw one modeled — these patterns persist because they were never examined. The Family Reckoning Arc provides the structured examination.

The arc is not family therapy. It works with one person — their experience, their memories, their regrets, their values, their decisions. It does not mediate between family members. It does not adjudicate competing versions of events. It helps one person get clear enough about their own experience and their own values to know what they want and to prepare to communicate it.

## Session Sequence

### Session 1: Family Conflict Navigation
**Pack:** family_conflict_navigation
**Purpose:** Map the family system and its conflicts. Who are the key players? What are the patterns? What are the current active conflicts and what are the historical ones that shaped the system? This session produces the family map that every subsequent session references.
**Vault writes:** family_map, active_conflicts, historical_patterns, family_roles, power_dynamics, alliances, estrangements

### Session 2: Memory Session
**Pack:** memory_session
**Purpose:** Work with family memories. Not a comprehensive family history — a targeted exploration of the memories that carry the most weight. The formative moments, the injuries, the unexpectedly tender moments, the memories the person has been avoiding. Honest memory work, not selective nostalgia.
**Vault reads:** family_map, historical_patterns from S1
**Vault writes:** key_family_memories, memory_themes, avoided_memories, memory_reappraisals

### Session 3: Regret Processing OR Self-Forgiveness
**Pack:** regret_processing OR self_forgiveness
**Routing:** If the person carries regret about their own role in family dynamics → regret_processing. If the person carries guilt or self-blame (often instilled by the family system itself) → self_forgiveness. Assessment from S1 and S2 determines the routing. Some people need both — in that case, this session may be repeated with the alternate pack.
**Purpose:** Address what the person carries — either regret about what they did or did not do, or guilt about who they are or are not. Family systems are expert at distributing blame, and the person's self-assessment may be the family's assessment, not their own.
**Vault reads:** key_family_memories, memory_themes from S2, family_roles from S1
**Vault writes:** regrets_named OR guilt_examined, processing_status, family_assigned_blame_vs_actual, self_compassion_status

### Session 4: Values Clarification
**Pack:** values_clarification
**Purpose:** After mapping the family system, working with memories, and processing regret or guilt — clarify what the person actually values now. Not what the family said they should value. Not what they absorbed by osmosis. What they, as an autonomous adult who has done the reckoning work, actually care about. This session often produces the most surprising results in the arc, because family values and personal values frequently diverge in ways the person has never examined.
**Vault reads:** family_map, family_roles from S1, regrets_named or guilt_examined from S3
**Vault writes:** personal_values_vs_family_values, values_inherited, values_rejected, values_chosen, values_in_tension

### Session 5: Family Meeting Prep
**Pack:** family_meeting_prep
**Purpose:** Prepare for the conversation. This may be a conversation with a parent, a sibling, the whole family, or no one — some people complete the reckoning and decide that the conversation is not worth having. That is a valid outcome. For those who choose to have the conversation, this session prepares them: what to say, what to expect, what to do when it goes sideways (because family conversations frequently do).
**Vault reads:** full arc vault
**Vault writes:** conversation_decision, conversation_plan, boundaries_defined, expected_reactions, contingency_plans, arc_reflection

- **S1 → S2:** historical_patterns — memory work is guided by the patterns identified in system mapping
- **S2 → S3:** key_family_memories, avoided_memories — regret/forgiveness work draws on the specific memories surfaced

- **All → S5:** full vault — conversation prep draws on everything: the map, the memories, the processing, and the clarified values

## Entry Session Protocol

The entry session assesses the family situation and screens for safety. Family of origin work can surface material that requires professional therapeutic support, and the entry session is responsible for identifying when that is the case.

Entry assessment captures:
1. **Family structure** — who is in the family, who is alive, who is in contact, who is estranged
2. **Presenting concern** — what is prompting this work now. A specific incident, a long-building pressure, a life transition that has made the family dynamics impossible to ignore
3. **Abuse and trauma screen** — direct question. Has there been physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, or substance abuse in the family system? If yes, the arc proceeds with awareness and routes to therapeutic support alongside.
4. **Current safety** — is the person currently in contact with the family? Is that contact safe?
5. **Desired outcome** — what does the person want from this work? Reconciliation, understanding, boundary-setting, estrangement with peace, or just clarity?

The entry assessment is written to vault. If abuse or trauma is disclosed, the arc proceeds with explicit acknowledgment and a strong recommendation for therapeutic support alongside. The arc does not process trauma. It provides structured reflection alongside professional care.

## Adaptation Rules

- Sessions 1-5 are sequential but S3 may be repeated (once with regret_processing, once with self_forgiveness) if both are needed.
- If abuse or trauma is disclosed at any point → acknowledge immediately. Do not minimize. Recommend therapeutic support alongside the arc. The arc may continue if the person has adequate professional support, but it does not replace it.
- If the person decides during the arc that they do not want to have a family conversation → S5 becomes a session about that decision. Choosing not to have the conversation is a decision that deserves the same preparation and clarity as choosing to have it.
- If family crisis erupts mid-arc (a death, a blowup, an estrangement) → the arc adapts. The current session addresses the crisis before continuing the sequence.
- If the person is simultaneously in family therapy → the arc complements, not competes. The therapist's direction takes precedence where they conflict.
- Pacing between sessions may be longer than other arcs. Family work is heavy. Weeks between sessions is normal and healthy.

## Completion Criteria

The arc is complete when:
1. The family system has been mapped and its patterns identified
2. Key memories have been examined honestly — including the ones that were avoided
3. Regret or guilt has been processed to the degree the person is ready
4. Personal values have been distinguished from family values — what is chosen vs. what was inherited
5. The person has decided what they want to do (conversation, boundary, estrangement, or continued reflection) and has either prepared for it or made peace with their decision

---

## Mandatory Routing

- **Abuse or trauma disclosure** → acknowledge, validate, recommend therapeutic support alongside. Do not minimize. Do not proceed as if this is ordinary family conflict.
- **Suicidal ideation** → crisis resources + stay present. Non-negotiable.
- **Current domestic violence** → safety resources immediately. The arc cannot proceed without safety established.
- **Child abuse currently occurring (the person reports knowledge of a child being abused)** → mandatory reporting obligations. Note this clearly. Encourage the person to report. Provide relevant resources.
- **Substance use as coping during the arc** → note and recommend professional assessment.
