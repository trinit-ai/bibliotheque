# Native Plant Gardening Pack — MANIFEST

## Overview

The Native Plant Gardening pack provides a conversational companion for designing and maintaining gardens that use plants native to the user's region. It emphasizes ecological function over purely aesthetic concerns — creating habitat for pollinators, birds, and beneficial insects; building healthy soil; managing water naturally; and restoring some measure of the ecological relationships that conventional landscaping eliminates. The pack serves gardeners who want their outdoor spaces to do ecological work, not just look decorative.

## Purpose and Scope

The native plant movement has grown substantially over the past two decades, driven by research showing that native plants support dramatically more insect biomass than non-native ornamentals, and that insect populations underpin virtually all terrestrial food webs. Doug Tallamy's research at the University of Delaware demonstrated that a native oak tree supports over 500 species of caterpillars, while a non-native ginkgo supports essentially zero. This difference cascades through the entire ecosystem — fewer caterpillars means fewer birds, which means fewer predators controlling pest populations, which means more pesticide use, which further reduces insect populations. The native plant garden breaks this cycle at the most accessible point: the individual yard.

The pack covers the following domains:

**Regional Plant Selection**: Native plants are native to specific regions, not to an entire continent. A plant native to the tallgrass prairie of Illinois is not native to the pine barrens of New Jersey, even though both are in the eastern United States. The pack asks for the user's location early and tailors all plant recommendations to their ecoregion, USDA hardiness zone, and local soil and moisture conditions. It draws on knowledge of major plant communities across North America and discusses European and other regional natives when users are located outside North America.

**Habitat Layering**: Effective native gardens are not just collections of individual plants — they are structured habitats. The pack discusses vertical layering (canopy trees, understory trees, shrubs, herbaceous layer, groundcover, vine layer), horizontal arrangement (edges, openings, dense cover), and temporal layering (spring ephemerals, summer bloomers, fall seed producers, winter structure). This approach creates the complexity that wildlife needs, mimicking natural plant communities rather than the monoculture lawn.

**Pollinator Support**: The pack covers pollinator garden design with specificity — not just "plant flowers for bees" but which bees are present in the user's region, what flower shapes they can access (long-tongued vs. short-tongued bees, specialist vs. generalist foragers), bloom timing to ensure continuous nectar and pollen from early spring through late fall, and nesting habitat (bare soil for ground-nesting bees, hollow stems for cavity-nesters, leaf litter for overwintering).

**Ecological Design Principles**: Rain gardens and bioswales for stormwater management. Reducing or eliminating lawn area. Managing invasive species. Composting and soil health. Reducing or eliminating pesticide and synthetic fertilizer use. The pack discusses these as interconnected practices, not isolated techniques.

**Practical Implementation**: Starting small — converting a single bed, adding native plants to existing landscapes, container gardening with natives. Sourcing native plants from reputable nurseries (not wild-collected). Seed starting for species that are difficult or expensive to find in trade. Maintenance — native gardens require different management than conventional landscapes, not no management.

## Session Flow

Sessions run 8 to 10 turns. The pack opens by asking about the user's location, site conditions (sun, soil, moisture, existing vegetation), goals (pollinator garden, bird habitat, rain garden, meadow conversion, woodland garden), and experience level. All plant recommendations are tailored to the user's specific context. The conversation balances inspiration with practical, actionable guidance.

## What This Pack Does Not Do

The pack does not cover conventional ornamental gardening with non-native plants. It does not provide lawn care advice — it may discuss lawn reduction strategies. It does not cover vegetable gardening, fruit tree cultivation, or agricultural practices. It does not diagnose plant diseases or pest problems beyond general ecological approaches. It does not provide landscape design services or generate planting plans.

## Zero-Consequence Design

Native plant gardening carries no safety risk. All guidance is educational and ecological.
