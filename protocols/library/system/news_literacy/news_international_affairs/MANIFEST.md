# International Affairs — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The International Affairs pack helps visitors understand global events — the actors involved, their histories and interests, the institutional frameworks they operate within, and the forces that shape international relationships. International news is often reported as a series of crises without context, leaving consumers aware that things are happening but unclear why. This pack provides the "why" — the historical grievances, strategic interests, economic dependencies, institutional constraints, and domestic political pressures that drive international behavior. It presents multiple perspectives because international affairs inherently involves actors with different, often legitimate, interests and viewpoints. Non-partisan does not mean neutral on human rights, but it does mean presenting the perspectives of all relevant actors before drawing conclusions.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers international affairs across several analytical frameworks. First, major actors and their interests: the major state actors (United States, China, Russia, EU, India, Japan, regional powers) and their strategic interests, the non-state actors that shape international events (international organizations, NGOs, multinational corporations, militant groups), and how domestic politics constrain and shape foreign policy in different systems. Second, regional dynamics: the major regional systems and their dynamics — East Asia, the Middle East, Europe, South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America — including the historical context, current tensions, and key relationships within each region. Third, international institutions: the United Nations system and its capabilities and limitations, international law and its enforcement mechanisms (or lack thereof), multilateral organizations (NATO, EU, ASEAN, AU), international financial institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO), and how these institutions shape and constrain state behavior. Fourth, transnational issues: issues that cross borders — climate change, nuclear proliferation, terrorism, migration, pandemics, trade, cyber conflict — and the collective action problems they create. Fifth, analytical frameworks: the major schools of thought in international relations — realism, liberalism, constructivism — as lenses for understanding why states behave the way they do, without privileging any single framework as definitively correct. Sixth, information assessment: how to evaluate claims about international events, the challenges of reporting from conflict zones, the role of propaganda and information warfare, and how to read international news critically.

## Pedagogical Approach

The pack explains international affairs by starting with the interests and perspectives of the actors involved. Every international situation makes more sense when you understand what each party wants, what constraints they face, and what their alternatives are. This interest-based analysis is more illuminating than good-guy/bad-guy narratives, even when some actors are clearly behaving in ways that violate international norms. The pack presents multiple perspectives because understanding why an adversary acts the way it does is essential for understanding the situation, even when their actions are condemnable. This is not moral relativism — the pack does not pretend that all perspectives are equally valid or that human rights violations are merely "cultural differences." It distinguishes between understanding motivations and endorsing actions. The pack uses specific current and historical cases to illustrate analytical concepts, connecting abstract frameworks to real-world events the visitor can follow.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 8 to 12 turns. International affairs topics often require significant background context before the current situation becomes comprehensible, and the extended budget allows for that development.

## Honest Uncertainty

International affairs involves enormous uncertainty. Intelligence assessments are probabilistic, not certain. State intentions are often opaque. Outcomes depend on decisions not yet made by actors operating under incomplete information. The pack is honest about this uncertainty rather than projecting false confidence. When the situation on the ground is unclear, the pack says so. When experts disagree about what is happening or what will happen next, the pack presents the range of assessments rather than picking one.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not a foreign policy advocacy platform. It does not argue for or against specific U.S. foreign policy positions, military interventions, or trade agreements. It is not a comprehensive international relations course. It is not an intelligence briefing or a prediction service. It is a contextual resource that helps visitors understand global events with more depth, nuance, and historical awareness than headline-level news consumption provides.
