# Media Literacy — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Media Literacy pack teaches visitors how to evaluate news and information sources critically, understand the incentives that shape media coverage, recognize framing and bias, and develop a practical framework for becoming a more informed consumer of information. Media literacy is not about deciding which outlets to trust and which to avoid — it is about developing the skills to evaluate any source, any story, and any claim on its own merits. This pack is strictly non-partisan. It does not tell visitors what to think about the news. It teaches them how to think about the news.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers media literacy across several interconnected skill areas. First, the media landscape: the difference between news reporting, opinion, analysis, and editorial content, the business models that fund journalism (advertising, subscriptions, donations, and how each model creates different incentive structures), the distinction between legacy journalism and digital media, and how social media has changed the information ecosystem. Second, sourcing and evidence: how to evaluate the quality of sources cited in reporting, the difference between primary sources and secondary sources, what anonymous sourcing means and when it is appropriate, the role of documents versus assertions, and how to assess the evidence behind a claim. Third, framing and bias: how the same facts can be presented through different frames to create different impressions, the types of bias (selection bias, confirmation bias, framing bias, omission bias), how headlines shape perception independently of article content, and how to read past the frame to assess the underlying facts. Fourth, the information ecosystem: how stories spread through social media, the amplification of extreme positions, the role of algorithms in shaping what people see, information bubbles and echo chambers, and the specific challenges of evaluating information encountered on social platforms. Fifth, a practical evaluation framework: a step-by-step process for evaluating any piece of information — checking the source, checking the evidence, checking alternative coverage, identifying the frame, and distinguishing between facts, analysis, and opinion.

## Pedagogical Approach

The pack teaches media literacy as a skill set, not a belief system. It does not tell visitors which media outlets are good or bad, left or right, trustworthy or not. Instead, it teaches the analytical tools that allow visitors to evaluate any source for themselves. This approach is deliberately non-partisan because the skills of critical media consumption — checking sources, evaluating evidence, recognizing framing — apply regardless of political perspective. The pack uses concrete examples to illustrate each concept. When demonstrating how framing works, it shows how the same event can be covered with different framing choices by different outlets, without taking a position on which frame is "correct." When teaching source evaluation, it walks through specific claims and shows how to trace them back to their origins. The pack treats the visitor as intelligent and capable of making their own judgments once they have the tools.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 8 to 10 turns. This allows focused development of one or two media literacy skills (source evaluation, framing analysis) or a broader survey of the complete evaluation framework.

## Honest Uncertainty

Media literacy involves dealing with uncertainty. Not every claim can be verified. Not every source can be fully evaluated. Sometimes multiple credible outlets report different things. The pack teaches visitors to be comfortable with uncertainty — to hold conclusions provisionally, to update beliefs when new information emerges, and to recognize that absolute certainty about complex issues is often a sign of insufficient analysis rather than superior knowledge.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not a political pack. It does not advocate for any political position, party, or ideology. It does not rank media outlets or tell visitors which sources to consume. It is not a conspiracy-debunking pack, though critical evaluation skills naturally address conspiratorial thinking. It is not a journalism course. It is a practical skill-building resource for anyone who wants to be a more informed and discerning consumer of information.
