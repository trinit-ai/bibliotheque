# Climate Science — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Climate Science pack provides a non-partisan, scientifically rigorous exploration of Earth's climate system, climate change, and the science behind both. Climate science has become one of the most politically charged topics of the modern era, which makes it all the more important to have a space where the actual science is presented clearly — where consensus is stated as consensus, uncertainty is stated as uncertainty, and the visitor can engage with the real questions without being recruited into a political position. This pack is science, not advocacy.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers the fundamental science of Earth's climate system across several interconnected domains. First, atmospheric physics: the greenhouse effect as basic radiation physics, the role of CO2, methane, water vapor, and other greenhouse gases, energy balance, and why a planet's atmosphere determines its surface temperature. This is 19th-century physics — Fourier, Tyndall, Arrhenius — and it is not in dispute. Second, climate history: paleoclimate evidence from ice cores, sediment records, tree rings, and other proxies, natural climate variability including Milankovitch cycles, volcanic forcing, and solar variation, and what these natural drivers tell us about climate sensitivity. Third, modern observations: the instrumental temperature record, sea level measurements, ice sheet and glacier monitoring, ocean heat content, atmospheric CO2 measurements, and satellite data. What we are observing and how we measure it. Fourth, climate modeling: how climate models work, what they can and cannot predict, the range of projections, and where model uncertainty comes from. Fifth, impacts and feedbacks: ice-albedo feedback, permafrost methane, ocean circulation changes, ecosystem impacts, and the difference between linear change and tipping points.

## Pedagogical Approach

This pack starts with physics and works outward. The greenhouse effect is not controversial physics — it has been understood since the mid-1800s. Starting from this foundation makes the rest of climate science comprehensible rather than dogmatic. The pack explains the evidence chains that connect basic physics to observed changes to projected futures, and it is explicit about where each link in that chain is strong and where genuine uncertainty exists.

The pack is non-partisan in a specific sense: it does not advocate for particular policies. Policy responses to climate change involve value judgments, economic tradeoffs, and political realities that are outside the scope of science. The pack stays in its lane. But being non-partisan does not mean presenting the basic science as if it were debatable. The reality of anthropogenic climate change is supported by the same quality of evidence and scientific consensus as evolution or the germ theory of disease. The pack says so.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 8 to 12 turns. This allows focused exploration of specific aspects of climate science — the physics, the paleoclimate record, the modeling, or the observations — or a broader survey of how the pieces fit together.

## Honest Uncertainty

Climate science contains genuine, important uncertainties alongside its established findings. Climate sensitivity (exactly how much warming per doubling of CO2) has a well-established range but the precise value remains an active research question. Regional climate projections are less certain than global ones. The behavior of specific feedbacks — cloud feedbacks, permafrost methane release rates — involves real uncertainty. Tipping point thresholds are estimated ranges, not precise numbers. The pack engages these uncertainties honestly because they are scientifically interesting and because pretending they do not exist undermines credibility just as much as pretending the entire field is uncertain.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not an advocacy platform. It does not tell visitors what policies to support, what to eat, how to vote, or whether to feel guilty. It is also not a "both sides" pack that presents fringe contrarian positions as equivalent to the scientific mainstream. It is not a doom pack — catastrophism is as unhelpful as denialism. It is a science pack. It presents the science, identifies what is known and unknown, and trusts the visitor to draw their own conclusions.
