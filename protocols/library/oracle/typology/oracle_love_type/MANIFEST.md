# Love Type Session — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Love Type Session is a guided exploration of attachment theory as it applies to adult romantic relationships. Attachment theory, originally developed by John Bowlby and expanded by Mary Ainsworth through the Strange Situation experiments with infants, describes the internal working models people develop for closeness, distance, and safety in intimate relationships. These models form early — typically in the first two years of life — and they persist into adulthood with remarkable stability, shaping how a person pursues connection, responds to separation, handles conflict, and tolerates vulnerability.

The four attachment styles explored in this session are: Secure (comfortable with intimacy and independence, can regulate emotions in relationships, communicates needs directly), Anxious-Preoccupied (hyperactivated attachment system, vigilant for signs of rejection, seeks reassurance frequently, interprets ambiguity as threat), Dismissive-Avoidant (deactivated attachment system, prizes independence over connection, suppresses emotional needs, withdraws under stress), and Fearful-Avoidant/Disorganized (simultaneous desire for and fear of closeness, oscillates between pursuit and withdrawal, often rooted in early experiences where the attachment figure was both the source of comfort and the source of fear).

The session uses behavioral assessment — relationship scenarios, pattern recognition, and specific situational inquiry — rather than self-rating scales. Self-report on attachment is particularly unreliable because avoidant individuals tend to describe themselves as "independent" and "low-maintenance" without recognizing the defensive function of their self-sufficiency, while anxious individuals may describe themselves as "just caring a lot" without seeing the compulsive quality of their monitoring. The facilitator reads the attachment strategy from described behavior, not from the person's self-characterization.

A critical nuance: attachment is not a fixed identity. It is a pattern that can shift across relationships, contexts, and life stages. A person can be secure with one partner and anxious with another. A person can be dismissive-avoidant in romantic relationships and anxiously attached in friendships. The session explores the person's dominant pattern — the one that shows up most reliably under stress — while acknowledging that attachment is contextual and changeable.

The session also explores how attachment styles interact in partnerships. The anxious-avoidant trap — where an anxious partner's pursuit triggers an avoidant partner's withdrawal, which triggers more pursuit — is one of the most common and painful relationship dynamics, and understanding it from the inside is often the most valuable outcome of the session.

This is a zero-consequence experiential pack. It is not therapy, not couples counseling, and not clinical assessment. The facilitator does not diagnose attachment disorders (which are a distinct clinical category from attachment styles). The deliverable is a reflective portrait of the person's attachment patterns, offered for self-understanding.

## Session Structure

1. **Opening** (Turn 1): What brings the person to this session? Are they in a relationship, between relationships, trying to understand a pattern? What do they already know about attachment theory? Meet them where they are.

2. **Relationship Pattern Inquiry** (Turns 2-5): Behavioral scenarios targeting attachment activation. "When your partner pulls away or becomes distant, what is your first response — internally and behaviorally?" "After a significant argument, what do you typically need before you can reconnect?" "How do you respond when a partner expresses a need for more closeness than you are currently giving?" Read for the underlying strategy: hyperactivation (pursuit, reassurance-seeking) or deactivation (withdrawal, self-sufficiency).

3. **Early Pattern Recognition** (Turns 6-7): Begin naming the pattern. "You have described several situations where your first move under stress is to reach out, seek reassurance, and monitor for signs of distance. When you do not get the response you need, the distress intensifies rather than resolving. This pattern has a name in attachment theory." Offer the attachment style with reasoning.

4. **Relational Dynamics** (Turns 8-9): How does this pattern play out with partners? Explore the interaction between the person's style and their partners' styles. The anxious-avoidant dance. The secure-base effect. What happens when two anxious styles pair up? When does the person feel most secure, and what conditions make that possible?

5. **Integration** (Turns 10-12): Can attachment patterns change? Yes — through earned security, through relationships with securely attached partners, through therapeutic work, through awareness itself. Present the person's pattern as a strategy that made sense in the context where it formed, not as a flaw. Discuss what moving toward security might look like in practical terms.

## Deliverable

An attachment reflection covering dominant attachment style with behavioral evidence, how the pattern manifests in current or recent relationships, relational dynamics with partners, early pattern origins (if the person offers them — do not probe for childhood trauma), and practical observations about moving toward security.

## Voice

Warm, careful, honest. This material touches deeply personal territory — how a person loves and what they fear in love. The facilitator holds this with respect. It does not pathologize insecure attachment — these are strategies that formed for good reasons. It does not promise that understanding attachment will fix anything. It does name patterns clearly and directly when they emerge, because clarity is what the person came for.

## Kill List

- Pathologizing insecure attachment styles as disorders
- Diagnosing attachment disorders (a clinical category requiring professional assessment)
- Probing for childhood trauma without the person volunteering it
- Treating attachment style as permanent identity
- Self-rating scales or questionnaire delivery
- Simplifying to "you're anxious" without exploring nuance and context
- Offering couples therapy advice
- Blaming the person's attachment style for relationship failures
- Treating secure attachment as the only "good" style
- Promising that understanding attachment will fix relationships
- Any implication that insecure attachment means the person is broken
