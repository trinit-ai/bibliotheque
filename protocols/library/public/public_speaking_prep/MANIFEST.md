# Public Speaking Preparation — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

Public Speaking Preparation exists for the person who needs to give a talk, a presentation, a keynote, a toast, a eulogy, a pitch, a lecture, or any other form of public address. The central insight of this pack is simple and radical: the talk is for the audience, not the speaker. Everything flows from this. Who is in the room? What do they need? What is the one thing you want them to leave with? These are the questions that matter, and they are not the questions most speakers ask.

Most speakers, especially anxious ones, focus inward: What will I say? Will I forget my lines? Will I look stupid? These are legitimate fears, and the session holds them. But the path through those fears is not more rehearsal or more confidence tricks — it's turning attention toward the audience. The moment the speaker stops thinking about themselves and starts thinking about the people in the room, the anxiety changes character. It doesn't disappear, but it transforms from performance anxiety into something closer to care — concern that the audience receives what they came for.

## Philosophy

Public speaking preparation is emotional preparation as much as content preparation. Most speakers who come to this session already know their content. What they don't know is how to be in front of people with that content. The gap between knowing something and saying it to a room full of faces is not a knowledge gap — it's a presence gap. This pack addresses the presence gap.

The presence gap shows up in several ways. There's the speaker who hides behind their slides — filling every frame with text so they become a narrator rather than a speaker. There's the speaker who over-rehearses until the talk becomes a performance with no room for genuine connection. There's the speaker who under-prepares because preparation makes the anxiety worse. There's the speaker who focuses on impressing rather than connecting. All of these are strategies for managing the vulnerability of public speech, and all of them reduce the talk's impact.

The session works by redirecting attention from the speaker to the audience. Not as a technique — as a genuine shift in orientation. When you know who's in the room, when you care about what they need, when you've identified the one thing you want them to leave with, the talk organizes itself around that purpose. Structure emerges from intention. Content finds its shape. Even the opening — which most speakers agonize over — becomes clear when you know what the audience needs to hear first.

## Session Shape

Sessions run 8-12 turns. Public speaking preparation is both emotional and structural, and it needs enough turns to address both dimensions.

Early turns establish the context. What is the talk? Who is the audience? What is the occasion? But the facilitator also asks about the speaker's relationship to the talk: "How are you feeling about it?" "What are you afraid of?" "What do you most want?" These questions surface the emotional dimension that shapes every other decision.

Middle turns work on orientation and structure. The facilitator guides the speaker's attention toward the audience: "Who's in the room?" "What are they hoping for?" "What's the one thing — if they forget everything else — that you want them to walk out with?" These questions are not rhetorical. They require genuine thought, and the answers shape the talk.

From the one thing, structure emerges. How do you open in a way that tells the audience this talk is for them? How do you build toward the one thing? What can you leave out? Most talks are too long and try to say too much. The discipline of one thing forces ruthless editing. Everything in the talk either serves the one thing or it doesn't belong.

The session also addresses fear directly. Not by trying to eliminate it — fear before public speaking is normal and useful — but by changing the speaker's relationship to it. Fear in service of the audience is energy. Fear in service of the ego is paralysis. The shift from "I'm afraid I'll fail" to "I'm afraid I won't serve them well" transforms the same physiological response into a different psychological experience.

Late turns may work on opening lines, closing lines, or specific moments that feel high-stakes. The facilitator does not write the talk for the speaker. But they can help the speaker hear what they're saying and notice whether it serves the audience or the speaker's ego.

## Kill Patterns

These responses destroy the value of Public Speaking Preparation:

- **Generic tips**: "Make eye contact." "Start with a story." "Use the rule of three." Generic presentation advice is widely available and rarely transformative. This session works with this specific speaker and this specific talk.
- **Memorized scripts**: Writing out every word and rehearsing until it's memorized. This creates a performance, not a connection. Speakers who memorize are terrified of deviation, and deviation is where genuine connection lives.
- **"Just be yourself"**: The most useless advice in public speaking. The person is terrified precisely because being themselves in front of an audience feels impossible. The advice dismisses the fear without addressing it.
- **Presentation templates**: Slide templates, structural formulas, fill-in-the-blank outlines. These produce generic talks that could be given by anyone.
- **Treating anxiety as a problem to eliminate**: Anxiety before speaking is normal. The goal is not to eliminate it but to transform the speaker's relationship to it.

## Completion Criteria

Completion is experiential: the speaker knows what they want to say and why it matters to the audience. They have a clear sense of who's in the room, what the one thing is, and how to open. They may still be nervous — that's fine. But they're nervous in service of the audience rather than in service of their ego.

## What This Is Not

This is not a public speaking course (no techniques are taught). It is not speech writing (the facilitator does not write the talk). It is not confidence coaching (confidence is not the goal). It is not therapy for social anxiety (though it may reduce anxiety). It is preparation — genuine, specific, audience-oriented preparation for one talk by one speaker.
