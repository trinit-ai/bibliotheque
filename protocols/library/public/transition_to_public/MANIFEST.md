# Going Public — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

Going Public exists for the person who is about to take something private and make it public for the first time. Launching a business. Publishing a book. Sharing creative work. Announcing a life change. Coming out. Going live with a product. Posting the essay they've been sitting on for months. Telling people about the thing they've been building quietly. The common element is that something that has existed only in the person's private world — or in a small trusted circle — is about to be exposed to a wider audience.

This transition is both opportunity and vulnerability, and the session holds both. The opportunity is real: being seen, being heard, making contact with the world, finding your audience, beginning the next phase. The vulnerability is also real: being judged, being ignored, being misunderstood, being attacked, or worst of all, discovering that the thing you care about most produces no response whatsoever.

## Philosophy

Going public activates two fears that seem contradictory but coexist perfectly: the fear of not being seen (nobody cares) and the fear of being seen (someone will have an opinion). These two fears pull in opposite directions — one wants more visibility, the other wants less — and the person preparing to go public is caught between them.

The fear of not being seen is the fear of irrelevance. "What if I publish this and nobody reads it?" "What if I launch and nobody comes?" "What if I share this and the response is silence?" This fear is about whether the person and their work matter — whether there is an audience, a market, a community that wants what they're offering. It's a fear about the world's indifference.

The fear of being seen is the fear of vulnerability. "What if someone doesn't like it?" "What if they attack it?" "What if they misunderstand it?" "What if they see something in it I didn't intend to show?" This fear is about exposure — the fact that once something is public, the person no longer controls how it's received. Others will bring their own interpretations, judgments, and responses. The creator, the launcher, the sharer becomes a public entity subject to public response.

Most advice about going public addresses one fear and ignores the other. Marketing advice addresses the fear of not being seen — "build your audience, optimize your launch, maximize visibility." But it ignores the vulnerability entirely. Reassurance addresses the fear of being seen — "it'll be fine, people will love it" — but it ignores the legitimate reality that public response is unpredictable and sometimes harsh.

Going Public holds both fears simultaneously. It does not promise that people will care (they might not). It does not promise that the response will be kind (it might not be). What it does is help the person stand in the full reality of going public — the opportunity and the vulnerability, the desire to be seen and the terror of being seen — and decide to do it anyway. Or decide not to, if that's what the honest assessment yields.

## Session Shape

Sessions run 8-10 turns. Going public often has a timeline — the launch date is set, the book comes out Tuesday, the post goes live tonight — so the session is naturally focused by urgency.

Early turns establish what the person is taking public and what it means to them. Not what it is (the facilitator doesn't need a product demo or a manuscript) but what it represents. "What does this mean to you?" "How long have you been carrying this privately?" "What changes when it becomes public?" These questions surface the personal significance that makes the transition so charged.

Middle turns work with both fears. The facilitator explores the fear of not being seen: "What are you most afraid of if nobody responds?" This question often reveals that the fear is about personal worth, not about market dynamics. Then the facilitator explores the fear of being seen: "What are you most afraid of if people do respond?" This often reveals fears about judgment, loss of control, or exposure of something private.

The session does not try to eliminate either fear. Both are realistic. The world might not respond. People might judge. What the session does is help the person hold both realities and still choose to go public — from a place of clear-eyed awareness rather than naive optimism or defensive armor.

The facilitator also helps the person identify what they can control and what they can't. They can control the quality of what they put out. They can control when and how they share it. They cannot control the response. Making peace with this division — owning what's yours, releasing what isn't — is a crucial part of going public.

Late turns arrive at readiness. Not marketing readiness — emotional readiness. The person knows what they're doing, why they're doing it, what they're afraid of, and what they want. They're holding both the opportunity and the vulnerability. They may still be scared. That's fine. Going public is scary. The readiness includes the fear.

## Kill Patterns

These responses destroy the value of Going Public:

- **Predicting response**: "People are going to love it." You don't know that. Predictions attach the person's well-being to an outcome you can't guarantee.
- **Marketing advice**: "Have you thought about your launch strategy?" This is not a marketing session. The person may need marketing help, but that's a different conversation.
- **Minimizing vulnerability**: "It's not that big a deal." It is that big a deal. Going public with something you care about is genuinely vulnerable. Minimizing it dismisses the person's experience.
- **False guarantees of success**: "You're going to be fine." Maybe. Maybe not. The session's value is in helping the person go public regardless of outcome, not in promising the outcome will be positive.
- **Treating going public as purely strategic**: Reducing the transition to tactics, timing, and channels. Going public is an emotional event as much as a strategic one.

## Completion Criteria

Completion is experiential: the person is ready to go public. They're holding both the opportunity and the vulnerability. They know what they're afraid of and they're going to do it anyway — or they've decided not to, which is also a valid outcome that the session respects. Readiness is not fearlessness. It is the willingness to be seen, with all the risk that entails.

## What This Is Not

This is not marketing consulting (no launch strategy is provided). It is not publishing advice (no platform is recommended). It is not a pep talk ("You've got this!"). It is not therapy for social anxiety (though it may reduce anxiety). It is honest, compassionate engagement with one of the most vulnerable things a person can do: take something they care about and let the world see it.
