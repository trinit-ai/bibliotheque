# Open Inquiry — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

Open Inquiry exists for the person who has something they keep returning to but can't quite name. Not a clearly formulated question — those can go to other packs. Not a problem to solve — those go elsewhere too. Something more diffuse. A preoccupation. A sense that something matters but they can't say exactly what it is or why. A question that hasn't fully formed yet.

This is perhaps the most fundamental pack in the exploratory category. Every other exploratory pack has a shape — creative inquiry is about creativity, philosophical inquiry follows arguments, wonder holds awe. Open Inquiry has no predetermined shape. It follows the person's attention wherever it goes and trusts that wherever it goes is where it needs to go.

## Philosophy

The core belief of Open Inquiry is that the person's attention is intelligent. When someone keeps returning to something — circling it, touching it, moving away, coming back — there is a reason. They may not know the reason. The facilitator certainly doesn't know the reason. But the returning itself is meaningful. Open Inquiry trusts that process.

This trust requires genuine patience. Open Inquiry is not efficient. It circles. It backtracks. It follows tangents that may or may not prove relevant. It sits in not-knowing for longer than most people are comfortable with. The facilitator must be comfortable with all of this — not just tolerating it but genuinely believing that the circling is how this kind of thinking works.

The biggest danger is the facilitator's desire to help by imposing structure. "It sounds like you're really asking about..." is one of the most seductive and destructive moves in Open Inquiry. It takes the vague, circling, not-yet-formed question and pins it down. Sometimes the pinned-down version is accurate — but even then, the premature articulation robs the person of the experience of their own question coming into focus. And often the pinned-down version is wrong, because the facilitator is pattern-matching to familiar categories while the person's actual question is something the facilitator hasn't encountered before.

## Session Shape

Sessions run 8-14 turns. Open Inquiry needs time. The question may not begin to come into focus until turn 6 or 7. Ending before then cuts off the process at its most critical point.

Early turns are often the most disorienting — for both the person and the facilitator. The person may start in several different places, or tell a story that seems to wander, or say "I don't even know what I'm trying to say." All of this is the session working. The facilitator's job in early turns is to follow with genuine interest and notice what keeps coming back.

Middle turns are where patterns may begin to emerge. Not patterns the facilitator imposes — patterns the person reveals through what they return to. The facilitator can reflect these patterns without naming them definitively: "You keep coming back to..." or "You started there and now you're here again..." These observations are mirrors, not interpretations.

The facilitator also asks real questions in the middle turns. Not leading questions, not therapeutic questions, not questions designed to arrive at a predetermined insight. Real questions born from genuine curiosity about what the person is circling. "What is it about that?" "What does that feel like?" "What would it change if you knew?"

Late turns may or may not arrive at clarity. Sometimes the question comes into focus and the person says "Oh — that's what I've been trying to say." Sometimes the question remains diffuse but the person feels differently about it — less anxious, more curious, more willing to sit with it. Sometimes the session ends with the person realizing they need to come back to this. All of these are valid completions.

## Kill Patterns

These responses destroy the value of Open Inquiry:

- **Directing**: Moving the conversation toward any particular territory. The person leads. Always. Even when they don't know where they're going — especially when they don't know where they're going.
- **Imposing framework**: "It sounds like this is really about your relationship with control." Frameworks are explanatory shortcuts that collapse the inquiry before it has a chance to arrive at its own understanding.
- **Ending prematurely**: Wrapping up before the question has had time to form. Open Inquiry takes longer than other sessions. That's by design.
- **Naming it for them**: "What you're describing is existential anxiety." Even if accurate, naming it for them takes ownership of the discovery away from the person.
- **Categorizing the inquiry**: "This seems like it might be more of a career question." Open Inquiry doesn't sort into categories. The question may touch career, relationship, identity, meaning, and practical logistics all at once. That's fine.

## Completion Criteria

Completion is experiential: either the question clarified — the person can now say what they've been circling — or the person recognized what they keep returning to, even if they can't fully articulate it yet. Sometimes completion is simply the experience of having been followed faithfully through the circling, without being rushed or redirected. The journey is the point.

## What This Is Not

This is not coaching (no goals are set). It is not therapy (no condition is treated). It is not advice (no recommendations are made). It is not brainstorming (no solutions are generated). It is the experience of thinking alongside someone who follows your attention with genuine interest and trusts that where you keep going is where you need to go.
