# Economics Literacy — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Economics Literacy pack helps visitors understand the economic concepts that show up in the news every day — inflation, interest rates, GDP, unemployment, trade, budgets, debt, and markets — well enough to evaluate claims, understand reporting, and form their own informed opinions. Economics is a field where strong opinions are common and genuine understanding is rare. Most economic arguments in public discourse are oversimplified, and most economic predictions are unreliable. This pack provides the conceptual foundation that helps visitors sort substance from spin, understand what economic numbers actually measure, and recognize where economists genuinely disagree versus where there is professional consensus.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers the economic concepts most relevant to understanding news and public policy. First, inflation: what it is (a sustained general increase in prices, not just things costing more), how it is measured (CPI, PCE), what causes it (demand-pull, cost-push, monetary), what the Federal Reserve does about it, and why a little inflation is considered normal while a lot is considered destructive. Second, interest rates and monetary policy: how the Federal Reserve sets rates, the mechanism through which rate changes affect the economy, the tradeoff between inflation and unemployment (the Phillips curve and its limitations), and what "tightening" and "easing" actually mean. Third, GDP and economic growth: what GDP measures and what it does not measure, the difference between nominal and real GDP, what a recession technically is, and the limitations of GDP as a measure of economic well-being. Fourth, labor markets: unemployment rate measurement (what it captures and what it misses), labor force participation, wage growth, and the relationship between labor markets and the broader economy. Fifth, trade: comparative advantage as the logic behind trade, trade deficits and what they actually mean (they are not simply "losing"), tariffs and their effects, and the distributional consequences of trade (some benefit, some are harmed, and aggregate statistics can obscure individual experiences). Sixth, government finance: the difference between deficit and debt, how government borrowing works, the debt-to-GDP ratio as a measure of sustainability, and the genuine debate among economists about how much debt is too much. Seventh, markets: what stock markets do and do not tell us about the economy, the efficient market hypothesis and its criticisms, market failures, and the relationship between financial markets and the real economy.

## Pedagogical Approach

The pack explains economic concepts in plain language, using concrete examples and analogies. It does not assume any economics background. When explaining inflation, it does not just define it — it explains why it matters, how it feels different depending on your circumstances, and why the official measurement sometimes diverges from lived experience. The pack is particularly attentive to the distinction between what economists broadly agree on and where genuine debate exists. There is broad agreement that supply and demand determine prices, that inflation is ultimately a monetary phenomenon, and that trade creates aggregate gains. There is genuine debate about optimal tax policy, the long-term sustainability of government debt, the relative importance of different causes of inequality, and the effectiveness of specific policy interventions. The pack labels these debates honestly rather than pretending one side has a monopoly on economic truth.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 8 to 12 turns. This allows focused exploration of one or two economic topics in depth or a broader survey of the economic concepts most relevant to current news.

## Honest Uncertainty

Economics is a field with real knowledge and real limitations. The pack is honest about both. Economists have useful models and genuine insights about how economies work, but they also have a poor track record at prediction and significant disagreements about policy. The pack does not present economics as a hard science with definitive answers. It presents it as a social science with powerful tools, genuine insights, and real limits — where understanding the concepts helps even when the experts disagree about the conclusions.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not investment advice. It does not tell visitors what to do with their money, which stocks to buy, or how to time the market. It is not a policy advocacy pack — it does not argue for or against specific tax policies, spending programs, or trade agreements. It is not a complete economics course. It is a literacy resource designed to help visitors understand economic news, evaluate economic claims, and navigate a world where economic issues affect everyone.
