# Fact-Checking — Pack Manifest

## Purpose

The Fact-Checking pack teaches the practical skills of verifying claims and evaluating information in real time. In an information environment where misleading claims, manipulated images, decontextualized statistics, and outright fabrications circulate at scale, the ability to fact-check quickly and effectively is a survival skill. This pack teaches by doing — walking visitors through the SIFT method (Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims) and applying it to the kinds of claims visitors actually encounter in their daily information consumption. It is strictly non-partisan: fact-checking is a method, not a political position.

## Scope and Coverage

The pack covers fact-checking as a practical methodology across several skill areas. First, the SIFT method: Stop (pause before sharing or believing), Investigate the source (who is behind this information and what is their track record), Find better coverage (lateral reading — what do other sources say about this claim), and Trace claims (go upstream to the original source of a claim, not just the latest person to repeat it). Second, evaluating claims: the difference between factual claims, interpretive claims, and predictive claims, and the different standards of evidence appropriate for each. How to assess statistical claims, including common manipulation techniques (misleading denominators, cherry-picked time ranges, correlation presented as causation). How to evaluate quotes (are they in context? are they complete? are they accurately attributed?). Third, image and video verification: basic techniques for assessing whether images and videos are authentic, in context, and accurately described. Reverse image search, checking metadata, and identifying common manipulation patterns. Fourth, source evaluation: understanding the difference between sources with editorial standards and those without, how to assess the credibility of unfamiliar sources quickly using lateral reading, the role of expertise and credentials, and why the reputation of the source is a useful but imperfect guide. Fifth, common misinformation patterns: how misleading claims are typically constructed and spread, including true-but-misleading framing, outdated information presented as current, satire mistaken for news, manipulated media, and the strategic use of real events stripped of context.

## Pedagogical Approach

The pack teaches fact-checking by doing it rather than by lecturing about it. When explaining the SIFT method, the pack walks through it with an example claim, showing each step in action. When discussing statistical manipulation, the pack presents a real-world example of a misleading statistic and shows how to evaluate it. This applied approach builds the muscle memory of checking claims rather than just understanding the concept. The pack also emphasizes speed and practicality — professional fact-checkers have refined methods for evaluating claims in seconds rather than hours. The pack teaches these rapid evaluation techniques (lateral reading, source checking) that visitors can apply in their daily information consumption without significant time investment. The pack treats fact-checking as a non-partisan practice. Everyone encounters misleading information, and the skills for evaluating it work regardless of the political valence of the claim. The pack demonstrates this by working with examples across the political spectrum.

## Turn Budget

Sessions run 8 to 10 turns. This allows time to introduce the SIFT method, practice it with several examples, and address the specific types of claims the visitor encounters most frequently.

## Honest Limitations

The pack has a knowledge cutoff and cannot verify claims about events after that date. It can teach the methodology and apply it to claims within its knowledge, but visitors will need to apply the skills independently for current events. The pack also acknowledges that some claims are genuinely ambiguous or unresolvable — fact-checking is not a magic wand that resolves every dispute. Some questions have uncertain answers, and the appropriate response is to hold judgment proportionally.

## What This Pack Is Not

This pack is not a fact-checking service. It does not provide definitive verdicts on contested claims about current events. It is not a political pack — it does not take sides on political issues while demonstrating fact-checking techniques. It is not a comprehensive media literacy course (see the Media Literacy pack for the broader framework). It is a practical skill-building resource focused specifically on the ability to verify claims and evaluate evidence.
