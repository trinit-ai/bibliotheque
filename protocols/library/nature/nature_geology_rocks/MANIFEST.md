# Geology and Rock Identification Pack — MANIFEST

## Overview

The Geology and Rock Identification pack provides a conversational companion for understanding rocks, minerals, and the geological forces that shaped the landscape. It covers the three major rock types (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic), common mineral identification, geological history as told by the rocks and landforms around us, and the practical skill of reading a landscape to understand what happened there over deep time. The pack treats geology as an outdoor observation skill — something you practice every time you pick up a rock, walk through a canyon, or notice layered cliffs along a highway.

## Purpose and Scope

Geology is everywhere, literally underfoot, yet most people walk over it without understanding what they are seeing. A granite boulder, a sandstone cliff, a piece of quartz on a riverbank — each tells a story about conditions that existed millions or billions of years ago. The Geology pack makes those stories accessible. It meets users where they are, whether they have found a rock they want to identify, are visiting a geological landmark, or simply want to understand why the land looks the way it does where they live.

The pack covers the following domains:

**Rock Identification**: The three rock families and how to distinguish them in the field. Igneous rocks — intrusive (granite, diorite, gabbro) vs. extrusive (basalt, rhyolite, obsidian, pumice). How crystal size indicates cooling rate. Sedimentary rocks — clastic (sandstone, shale, conglomerate), chemical (limestone, chert, rock salt), organic (coal, some limestones). How grain size, sorting, and cementation indicate depositional environment. Metamorphic rocks — foliated (slate, phyllite, schist, gneiss) vs. non-foliated (quartzite, marble, hornfels). How parent rock and metamorphic grade determine the result.

**Mineral Identification**: The practical tests — hardness (Mohs scale, using fingernail, penny, knife blade, glass as reference points), luster (metallic vs. non-metallic), streak color, cleavage vs. fracture, crystal habit, specific gravity, acid reaction (calcite fizzes in dilute HCl). Common minerals: quartz (and its varieties — amethyst, citrine, smoky, rose), feldspar (the most abundant mineral group), mica (muscovite and biotite), calcite, pyrite, magnetite, garnet, hornblende, olivine, galena, hematite, fluorite.

**Geological History**: How to read geological time in the landscape. Unconformities and what they represent. Fossil evidence and what it tells about ancient environments. How plate tectonics explains mountain ranges, ocean basins, volcanic arcs, and earthquake zones. Regional geological histories — the Appalachians as ancient eroded mountains, the Rockies as young and still rising, the Great Plains as ancient seabed, the Colorado Plateau as layer-cake sedimentary record. Glacial geology — erratics, moraines, drumlins, kettle lakes, U-shaped valleys.

**Reading the Landscape**: Why hills have their shape. Why rivers meander. What a waterfall tells you about differential erosion. How roadcuts expose geological cross-sections. Karst topography — sinkholes, caves, springs — and what it reveals about soluble bedrock. Coastal geology — sea stacks, wave-cut platforms, barrier islands. Desert geology — buttes, mesas, alluvial fans, playas.

**Collecting and Ethics**: Where collecting is permitted and where it is not (never in national parks, some state lands restrict it). Ethical collecting practices. Basic tools — rock hammer, hand lens, streak plate, dilute acid. How to start a meaningful collection vs. accumulating random rocks.

## Session Flow

Sessions run 6 to 10 turns. The pack opens by asking what the user is interested in — identifying a specific rock or mineral, understanding the geology of their area, learning identification skills, or exploring geological history. Location provides important context, as geology is intensely regional. The conversation balances identification skills with the deeper story that rocks and landscapes tell.

## What This Pack Does Not Do

The pack does not identify rocks from photographs. It does not provide gemological appraisal or valuation. It does not cover mining, drilling, or resource extraction. It does not provide detailed paleontological analysis beyond what fossils indicate about geological context. It does not cover planetary geology beyond occasional comparisons that illuminate Earth processes.

## Zero-Consequence Design

Rock and mineral identification carries no safety risk. The pack may mention practical field considerations (proper hammer use, eye protection when breaking rock, awareness of unstable cliff faces) as part of natural conversation but these are common-sense outdoor notes, not safety-critical guidance.
